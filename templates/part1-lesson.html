Lesson 1

The first lesson consists of the Arabic letters alif, waw, and yaa. After you master (1) the shape, (2) the signs, and (3) how to write them, then time for the exercises. Alif gives the sounds of the A in N avid or Obama, and also the sound of A in man. But not the normal sound of A like in gay, smile! Alif is straight not gay. When it comes in the beginning of Word it gets a glottal stop mark above it. Which is the controversial hamza. For now just know that because of pronouncing a stop with the throat when alif comes in start of Word, we mark it with this hamza, like the weird Apple. But it can come below the alif like the word Islam. Notice the difference in sound of the throat or glottal stop.

Exercises

Now you can write man and Navid, substituting the A with alif.

Now for the wow sound, its shape is a whirl. Its name is waaw, and the A is like the one in obama. It looks like a 9. When it comes at the beginning of a word it is pronounced "wu" like in woman. Or "wo" like in wall. So remember Alif with man and waaw with wuman! Notice the curves, smile. it comes in the middle or end of a word it loses the W, and becomes just a U or an O.

Exercises

Write woman in English but substitute the "wo" with waaw. Write judo and week. Now write this first word all in Arabic, wow. If you could write it then you had learned successfully the first and most important two letters/vowels of Arabic.

The final vowel is Yaa'. Which is the last letter of Arabic. It looks like a Y inverted 90 degrees. And it actually is a Y when it comes in the start of a word. The two dots below it, you can remember from the two dots in the Yin and Yang symbol. When it comes in the beginning it is a Yaa or Yuu or Yo depending on the diacritic symbols written above or below it (usually not written, these are the short vowel sounds, they are guessed from context). Which is actually the most confusing thing for a beginner. The vowels when they are short are not written. They are written only when they are clear sounding or emphasized or long. Back to yaa', it is pronounced Y in the beginning of a word, and E in the middle or end. Also it has one more special sound, which is the gay sound of A in mate, hate, nation, rate, blade. All those As are written with yaa. Focus on this because it confuses even many arabic speakers. End of lesson 1. Good luck.

Exercises

Write year, give, green, and guide. To wind up the vowels write: way, yo-yo, yaw, wowy, and the name of the letter Y in English.

Lessons 5 & 6

Lesson 5, page 8: G. This letter has a dual nature, like the English G. The English G is pronounced differently in the two words green and page. The Arabic standard pronunciation is J like in Japan. However in some dialects like Egyptian it is pronounced G like in green. So let's say both are correct and understandable, so choose whichever pronunciation from both. Note: there are 5 letters that can be connected only to the preceding letters, namely: alef, wow, and the others coming soon. Basically connecting letters is easy, you just use a dash to connect, going from right to left. But notice that usually the standalone (or a more accurate name, the full shape) shape of some letters changes a bit when it comes at the end of a word. Just by adding an open ring usually to the right or to the left. Like this G/j: ج. Or just جبل. When it is connected to the letter after it, it continues moving instead of closing with the curved ring. Try to use a pencil to write, don't type only.

Exercises

Write: gaat, gut, jeep, job, big, bag, and Togo. Note that the I in the beginning of a word like Islam is written with an alif, but below this alif is a hamza (glottal stop) to denote the E sound. Now write Egypt. Write: stop. swiss. site (write the vowels here for practice even if they are short). pizza=peetsa. space. goose. city. Write: saga. sub. substitute.

Lesson 6, page 9: S. That letter looks like a snake indeed both in Arabic and in English! Note how the standalone or final shape closes with a ring (a bit different ring than the G though but same idea). Now the letter SA. This letter is peculiar to Arabic. And I call it the heavy S. Listen to it on YouTube and you will see it is a magnified S.

Exercises

Write: stop, swiss, and site (write the vowels here for practice even if they are short). Write pizza=peetsa, space, goose, city, saga, sub, and substitute.
